On the Brink of Extinction

The Mauritius Kestrel (Falco punctatus) is a small bird of prey unique to the forests of Mauritius. For thousands of years, it thrived as an apex predator in its island ecosystem.

However, the 20th century brought disaster. Widespread deforestation decimated their natural habitat. The introduction of invasive species like macaques, mongooses, and feral cats led to the plundering of their nests. Most devastatingly, the use of the pesticide DDT caused their eggshells to thin and break before hatching.

1974: The Rarest Bird in the World

By 1974, the population had plummeted to an estimated four individuals, including just one breeding female. Scientists around the world considered the species doomed.

Enter Carl Jones and the Mauritian Wildlife Foundation. Through aggressive captive breeding, supplemental feeding, and providing predator-proof nest boxes, they achieved the impossible.

The Road to Recovery

A timeline of the intensive conservation efforts that saved the species.

Pre-1970s

Rapid Decline

Massive logging of ebony forests and widespread agricultural use of DDT pushes the Kestrel to the absolute brink.

1974

The Darkest Hour

Only 4 wild birds remain. Many experts believed the species was on the verge of extinction and unlikely to recover.

1979 - 1980s

Intensive Intervention

Conservationist Carl Jones arrives. He pioneers extreme techniques: climbing trees to harvest eggs, artificially incubating them, and hand-rearing chicks to release back into the wild.

2000s - Present

A Population Restored

The population stabilizes around 350-400 individuals. Ongoing efforts by the Mauritian Wildlife Foundation ensure nest boxes are maintained and predators are controlled.

Ecology & Behavior

Native Forests

They are entirely dependent on native Mauritian canopy forests. Their short, rounded wings allow them to dart nimbly between thick branches, unlike open-sky falcons.

Unique Diet

Unlike most kestrels that eat mammals, the Mauritius Kestrel feeds predominantly on endemic day geckos (Phelsuma), agamid lizards, and occasionally small birds or insects.

Nesting Habits

They do not build their own nests. Instead, they seek out natural cavities in old trees or cliff faces. Today, many rely on artificial nest boxes provided by conservationists.
